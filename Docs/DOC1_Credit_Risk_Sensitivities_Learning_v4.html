Credit Risk Sensitivities — Living Study Notes

A daily-read document to make curves, CDS, CS01, PFE, CVA, and SA-CVA stay connected.

1) Final intuition — lock this in

Risk-free curve → “time value of money” Credit spread curve → “how risky the borrower is” CDS → “instrument that tells you and trades that risk” Survival curve → “probability the borrower defaults”

2) The 3 lenses: one credit risk, three languages

Master insight: Yield, spread, CDS premium, and default probability are different languages describing the same underlying credit risk.

Lens 1 — Bond

Credit risk shows up as higher yield.

Example: if risk-free is 4% and ABC Corp bond yields 5%, the extra 1% is the credit spread.

Bond view: “I need 5% instead of 4% because ABC has credit risk.”

Lens 2 — CDS

Credit risk shows up as insurance premium.

Example: ABC 5Y CDS spread = 100 bp. A protection buyer pays 1% per year for default protection.

CDS view: “I pay 1% per year to insure against ABC default.”

Lens 3 — Survival Curve

Credit risk shows up as default probability.

Example: the same 100 bp spread can imply an approximate 5-year survival probability around 92%.

Survival view: “ABC has about an 8% chance of default over 5 years.”
Important correction: CDS does not “offset the spread curve.” CDS is an instrument whose value moves with the credit spread curve. A CDS protection position can be used to hedge a bond because the bond and CDS react in opposite directions when spreads widen.

3) Same trade priced 3 ways — the aha moment

Example: ABC Corp 5-year credit risk, notional = $1,000,000.

1. Bond View

Risk-free rate = 4% Credit spread = 1% Bond yield = 5% Price = discounted cashflows using 5% → Value ≈ $1,000,000

The bond expresses credit risk through extra yield.

2. CDS View

CDS spread = 100 bp Protection buyer pays 1% per year. Value depends on: - premium leg - protection leg At fair value: Premium ≈ Protection

The CDS expresses credit risk through insurance premium.

3. Survival Curve View

Spread = 100 bp Recovery = 40% Implied default intensity ≈ 1.67% 5Y survival probability ≈ ~92% Used to: - price CDS - compute CVA

The survival curve expresses credit risk through probability.

All 3 are different representations of the same thing: the market view of ABC Corp credit risk.

10) Exposure, EE, PFE, stressed PFE, and risk factors

Final intuition: Risk factors move → trade values change → exposure changes → EE / PFE / stressed PFE are calculated from those future exposure outcomes.

10.1 What are risk factors?

Risk factor Simple meaning Example movement What it can change
Interest rate curve Market interest rates over time 5Y USD rate moves from 4.00% to 4.25% Discounted value of cashflows
Credit spread curve Market view of borrower credit risk ABC 5Y spread moves from 100 bp to 130 bp Bond value, CDS value, CVA
FX rate Currency exchange rate EUR/USD moves from 1.10 to 1.05 Value of cross-currency trades
Equity price Stock/index price S&P 500 moves down 5% Equity derivatives
Risk factors are not the output. They are the moving market variables used to create future trade values.

10.2 What is exposure?

Exposure = “If the counterparty defaults at that time, how much do they owe me?”

Exposure is calculated after revaluing trades and applying netting rules.

Trade Future value to bank Meaning
Trade A +$1,050,000 Counterparty owes bank
Trade B -$800,000 Bank owes counterparty
Netting set exposure $250,000 Positive amount owed to bank after legal netting

10.3 What is an exposure profile?

An exposure profile is exposure measured across future time points.

Future time Expected Exposure / EE PFE Plain-English reading
1Y $300K $500K At 1Y, typical exposure is $300K, bad-but-plausible exposure is $500K
3Y $500K $700K At 3Y, exposure is expected to be larger
5Y $600K $900K At 5Y, the tail exposure is higher

10.4 EE, PFE, stressed PFE

Metric Simple meaning Example Used for
EE / Expected Exposure Average exposure across many simulated outcomes at a future time At 3Y, average exposure = $500K CVA calculation
PFE / Potential Future Exposure High-percentile exposure under ordinary simulation assumptions At 3Y, 95th percentile exposure = $700K Counterparty limits and risk reporting
Stressed PFE High-percentile exposure under crisis-like market conditions At 3Y, stressed 95th percentile exposure = $1.2M Stress testing, risk appetite, capital analysis
Important: Stressed market conditions are not the same thing as ordinary scenarios. Ordinary scenarios simulate many possible normal-market paths. Stressed market conditions deliberately force the market into crisis-like assumptions.

10.5 Ordinary scenarios vs stressed market conditions

Concept What it means Example Output
Ordinary scenarios Many simulated future paths based on normal market behavior Interest rates move up/down mildly; credit spreads move 5–30 bp EE and normal PFE
Stressed market conditions Deliberately severe market environment Credit spreads widen 150 bp, rates jump, FX moves sharply, volatility spikes Stressed PFE
Ordinary scenario example: ABC 5Y spread path: 100 bp → 110 bp → 120 bp Exposure at 3Y: $700K PFE Stressed market example: ABC 5Y spread shock: 100 bp → 250 bp Exposure at 3Y: $1.2M stressed PFE

11) CVA rebuilt using exposure intuition

CVA = expected loss from counterparty default. It combines exposure with default probability and recovery.

11.1 Keep the worlds separate

Exposure world: Risk factors move → trades are revalued → netting is applied → EE/PFE/stressed PFE Default world: Credit spreads → survival/default curve → probability of default CVA world: Expected Exposure + Default Probability + Loss Given Default → CVA

11.2 Step-by-step CVA with numbers

Future time EE used for CVA Incremental default probability Loss if default occurs Expected loss Discounted expected loss
1Y $300K 1.67% 60% of exposure About $3K About $2.9K
3Y $500K 3.00% 60% of exposure About $9K About $8.2K
5Y $700K 3.00% 60% of exposure About $12.6K About $10.4K
Total CVA About $21.5K

11.3 What each number means

Number Meaning Source
$300K / $500K / $700K EE Average exposure at future time points Exposure simulation after netting
Default probability Chance counterparty defaults in that period Credit spread / survival curve
60% loss If recovery is 40%, loss given default is 60% Recovery assumption
Discounted expected loss Expected loss converted into today’s dollars Risk-free discount curve
Daily-read version: CVA starts with expected exposure, then asks: “What is the chance the counterparty defaults, how much would I lose after recovery, and what is that loss worth today?”

12) Why SA-CVA uses sensitivities instead of exposure directly

Exposure / EE / PFE

These are model-heavy outputs.

  • Depend on simulation choices
  • Depend on netting and collateral
  • Depend on future market path assumptions
  • Difficult to compare across banks

Sensitivities

These are cleaner regulatory risk inputs.

  • Measure how CVA changes when market risk factors move
  • Can be bucketed by curve, tenor, counterparty, risk class
  • Can receive prescribed risk weights
  • Easier to aggregate with correlations

12.1 The key distinction

CVA: “How much expected loss do I have from counterparty default?” SA-CVA: “How much can my CVA change when market risk factors move?”

12.2 Example

Input Sample value Meaning
CVA amount $21.5K Expected loss adjustment today
CVA credit spread sensitivity +$20 per 1 bp If counterparty credit spread moves 1 bp, CVA changes by about $20
Risk weight 10% Regulatory scaling factor for the risk bucket
Weighted sensitivity 2 20 × 10%
Correlation 25% Used to combine multiple risk buckets without simply adding everything
Final intuition: CVA uses exposure. SA-CVA capital uses the sensitivity of CVA to market movements. That is why SA-CVA is a sensitivity-based capital framework, not just an exposure report.

13) Common Confusion — CS01 vs CVA CS01

Same spread bump (+1 bp) is used, but applied in two different engines.
Spread bump (+1 bp) ↓ ┌───────────────┬───────────────┐ │ │ │ ▼ ▼ ▼ Reprice trades Recompute CVA (same shock) │ │ ▼ ▼ Trade CS01 CVA CS01

13.1 Definitions

Concept Meaning
Trade CS01 Change in trade value when credit spread moves by 1 bp
CVA Expected loss from counterparty default
CVA CS01 Change in CVA when credit spread moves by 1 bp

13.2 Key takeaway

Same shock → different engines → different outputs Trade CS01 ≠ CVA CS01 Trade CS01 = price sensitivity CVA CS01 = risk (expected loss) sensitivity
We do NOT feed trade CS01 into CVA. We reapply the same shock and recompute CVA.

14) Market Risk, Risk Factors, and How Stress Impacts CCR

Core bridge: Market risk moves the risk factors. Those movements revalue trades. Revalued trades change exposure. Changed exposure changes counterparty credit risk and CVA.

14.1 The connected flow

Risk factors move ↓ Trades are repriced / MtM changes ↓ Netting is applied ↓ Exposure profile changes ↓ EE / PFE / stressed PFE change ↓ CVA and counterparty risk change

Market risk = the cause of market movement CCR = the counterparty exposure impact of that movement Markets move -> trades reprice -> exposure changes -> CVA changes Do not mix base and stressed numbers silently. Always label: scenario, time bucket, confidence level, and valuation date.
