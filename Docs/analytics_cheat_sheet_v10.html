1) The master mental model

Data product = governed package of data + rules Fact table = numeric core of the product Metric = named business number Grain = level at which the metric is true Dimension = business axis used to slice or roll the metric Hierarchy = valid navigation path Semantic layer = machine-readable rules for meaning, joins, and aggregation BI tool = user-facing interface that applies those rules
Key distinction: Documentation tells humans the rules. The semantic layer and BI model enforce the rules for machines and users.

Governance role of each concept ENRICHED

Each concept above plays a specific role in keeping numbers correct:

ConceptGovernance role

GrainThe contract for what the metric means — the level at which the number is true.

This mapping connects "concepts" to "what does this protect us from getting wrong."

2) Star schema vs galaxy / fact constellation

Star schema

One central fact table surrounded by dimensions.

dim_counterparty | fact_exposure | dim_date / dim_scenario / dim_time_bucket

Good when the analytics question is centered around one fact.

Galaxy schema (a.k.a. fact constellation)

Multiple facts share conformed dimensions.

dim_counterparty | fact_exposure fact_mtm fact_cva fact_limits fact_sensitivities

Needed for CCR because exposure, limits, MtM, CVA and sensitivities live at different grains.

Naming note: "galaxy schema" and "fact constellation" mean the same thing. Use whichever your audience knows. Industry has drifted slightly toward "galaxy" to avoid confusing "constellation" as a generic word.

Why CCR can't be a single star ENRICHED
Exposure lives at counterparty + netting set + time bucket + scenario. MtM lives at trade + valuation date. CS01 lives at trade + risk factor + tenor. These are not the same grain. A single fact table forcing them together would either lose detail or duplicate rows. The galaxy pattern lets each metric live at its true grain, joined by conformed dimensions when consumers ask cross-metric questions.

4) Data products and multi-grain design

Atomic products

  • Exposure product: fact_exposure
  • MtM product: fact_mtm
  • Sensitivities product: fact_sensitivities
  • CVA product: fact_cva and fact_cva_sensitivity
  • Limits product: fact_limits

These preserve source-of-truth grain and drill-down.

Curated constellation

Counterparty Risk 360 aligns metrics to a common grain.

grain = counterparty + valuation_date + scenario contains = EE, PFE, stressed PFE, MtM, collateral, limit, breach, CVA, CVA CS01

It is derived, governed and labeled; it does not replace atomic products.

Rule: Do not row-join fact_exposure to fact_sensitivities. Aggregate each fact to the requested common grain, then combine.

Three-way data product taxonomy REFINED

The atomic vs curated binary is useful, but industry recognises a three-way taxonomy that's more honest:

TypePurposeExample
Atomic / Source-of-truthPreserves grain and audit trail. Survives consumption changes.fact_exposure, fact_mtm, fact_sensitivities
Curated / 360 / SemanticPre-aggregated for a business question at a chosen grain.Counterparty Risk 360 (counterparty + valuation_date level)
Reporting / OperationalConsumption-ready for ops or regulatory submissions.Daily CCR Exposure feed, Regulatory SA-CCR submission
Important framing point REFINED
Counterparty Risk 360 is a derived constellation product — it sits on top of the four atomic products (Exposure, Valuation, Sensitivity, Limits). If Counterparty 360 is de-funded, the four atomic products keep delivering value. If any of the four atomic products is de-funded, the 360 view breaks. Atomic products are primary; curated products are layered on top.

5) Data contracts: relatable definition

A data contract says:
Here is the number I am giving you.
Here is exactly what it means.
Here is the grain where it is true.
Here is how you can roll it up.
Here is how you must not use it.
Here is who owns it.

MeaningHigh-percentile potential future exposure under ordinary simulation assumptions.

Without this row, contracts feel like they're about describing the data. The change-management row makes them about committing to the data. Consumers need to know what kind of stability they're getting before they build dependent pipelines.

Sharpened recommendation SHARPENED

Industry-converged opinion:

  • Source of truth in dbt or Databricks Metric View — logic lives once, in YAML, in version control, with tests.
  • Mirror in MicroStrategy — Strategy doesn't auto-import dbt or Databricks YAML; manual mapping is required.
  • Validate BI outputs against the source — regression tests catch drift between source-of-truth definitions and Strategy implementations.

Strong-form opinion:

  • dbt Metrics is the more mature pattern for source-of-truth metric definitions because (a) it's tool-agnostic, (b) it has the richest test framework, (c) it works across multiple BI tools.
  • Databricks Metric Views is younger but is the right answer if you're a single-Databricks shop and want to stay in Unity Catalog.
  • MicroStrategy can never be the source of truth if you have dbt or Databricks. Strategy is always downstream because its semantic model is locked inside the Strategy tool.

If TDS is committed to MicroStrategy: source of truth elsewhere (dbt or Databricks), mirrored in Strategy, with periodic regression tests proving they agree.

10.2 Unsafe query pattern — what NOT to do

The mistake is to join all fact tables row-by-row and then aggregate. This can duplicate numbers because the facts live at different grains.

-- BAD PATTERN: direct fact-to-fact joining SELECT c.counterparty_name, SUM(e.ee) AS total_ee, SUM(e.pfe) AS total_pfe, SUM(m.mtm_value) AS mtm, SUM(s.sensitivity_value) AS cs01 FROM fact_exposure e JOIN fact_mtm m ON e.counterparty_id = m.counterparty_id JOIN fact_sensitivities s ON e.counterparty_id = s.counterparty_id JOIN dim_counterparty c ON e.counterparty_id = c.counterparty_id WHERE c.counterparty_name = 'ABC Bank' GROUP BY c.counterparty_name;
Why this is wrong: fact_exposure may have one row per netting set and time bucket, fact_mtm may have many rows per trade, and fact_sensitivities may have many rows per trade/risk factor/tenor. Joining them directly can multiply rows and overstate metrics.

10.3 Safe query pattern — aggregate first, then combine

The semantic layer should aggregate each data product to the requested common grain first, then combine the summarized results.

-- GOOD PATTERN: aggregate each product first, then combine WITH exposure_summary AS ( SELECT counterparty_id, valuation_date, scenario_id, SUM(ee) AS total_ee, MAX(pfe) AS pfe, -- max across time buckets; -- consumers may want a specific bucket -- see semantic registry for chosen rule MAX(stressed_pfe) AS stressed_pfe FROM fact_exposure WHERE time_bucket = '5Y' GROUP BY counterparty_id, valuation_date, scenario_id ), limit_summary AS ( SELECT counterparty_id, valuation_date, MAX(limit_amount) AS limit_amount FROM fact_limits WHERE limit_type = 'PFE_LIMIT' GROUP BY counterparty_id, valuation_date ), mtm_summary AS ( SELECT counterparty_id, valuation_date, SUM(mtm_value_usd) AS total_mtm FROM fact_mtm GROUP BY counterparty_id, valuation_date ), sensitivity_summary AS ( SELECT counterparty_id, valuation_date, SUM(sensitivity_value_usd) AS total_cs01 FROM fact_sensitivities WHERE sensitivity_type = 'CS01' GROUP BY counterparty_id, valuation_date ) SELECT c.counterparty_name, e.valuation_date, e.total_ee, e.pfe, e.stressed_pfe, l.limit_amount, CASE WHEN e.pfe > l.limit_amount THEN 'BREACH' ELSE 'OK' END AS breach_flag, m.total_mtm, s.total_cs01 FROM exposure_summary e LEFT JOIN limit_summary l ON e.counterparty_id = l.counterparty_id AND e.valuation_date = l.valuation_date LEFT JOIN mtm_summary m ON e.counterparty_id = m.counterparty_id AND e.valuation_date = m.valuation_date LEFT JOIN sensitivity_summary s ON e.counterparty_id = s.counterparty_id AND e.valuation_date = s.valuation_date JOIN dim_counterparty c ON e.counterparty_id = c.counterparty_id WHERE c.counterparty_name = 'ABC Bank';

10.4 What the semantic layer is doing

Semantic responsibilityExample rule
Choose the correct data productExposure questions use fact_exposure; sensitivity questions use fact_sensitivities.

Two clarifications added CLARIFIED

(1) On MAX(pfe) being a semantic choice: The safe query uses MAX(pfe) across time buckets to produce a single point estimate. This is a semantic choice, not a technical one. Some consumers want PFE at a specific time bucket (e.g., 5Y). Some want the max across all buckets. Some want a curve. The choice belongs in the semantic registry — the SQL implements whatever the registry says.

(2) On the breach flag in the SELECT: Breach is a derived metric defined relative to a limit at a specific grain — counterparty + valuation_date in this case. It's computed in the SELECT (not a separate downstream step) so the grain context is preserved. If you computed breach at a different layer, later re-aggregation could silently invalidate it.

10.5 Data product discussion — layered architecture REFRAMED v9

A data product is not just a table. It is a governed package around a business concept. And critically, a "Counterparty 360" or any other 360-style data product is not a single wide table — it is a layered architecture of one summary product plus several atomic drill-down products, orchestrated by a BI tool or semantic layer at consumption time. This section makes that layering explicit.

Layer 3 — BI / Semantic orchestration (the user experience)

When a user types "ABC Bank" into the dashboard, the BI tool (MicroStrategy, Power BI, etc.) does this:

  1. Loads the Counterparty Risk Summary for that counterparty (Layer 1) — instant, one row, populates the top of the dossier.
  2. Loads tabs / drill-down panels on demand from Layer 2 atomic products, filtered by counterparty_id — only what the user clicks on.
  3. Each tab respects the natural grain of its source product. Trades tab shows trade-level rows; Exposure tab shows netting-set-and-bucket rows; Sensitivities tab shows trade-and-risk-factor rows.

The semantic layer ensures aggregations done within a tab (e.g., "show total CS01 by tenor") respect the rules from the data contract — SUM where additive, MAX/percentile where not.

Funding and dependency implications CARRIED FORWARD
Atomic primary products exist regardless of whether anyone builds the Layer 1 summary. The summary is layered on top of them. Funding decisions should reflect this dependency: fund the atomic products first; the summary is layered work that breaks if any of the underlying atomic products is de-funded. Without atomic products, there's nothing to drill down to from the summary; the dashboard becomes just a top panel with no detail.
The wide-table fallacy NEW v9
Many people picture a Counterparty 360 as a single wide table — one row per counterparty containing trades, exposures, sensitivities, collateral, all the metrics. This doesn't work. The facts live at different grains; forcing them into one wide row would require either Cartesian-multiplying the data (millions of rows per counterparty inquiry, with all the wrong-aggregation problems that creates) or losing all the trade-level detail. The correct pattern is one summary row + many atomic detail tables, linked by counterparty_id, orchestrated by the BI tool. See §15 for a full worked example.

11) Modelling schools — where each fits in this picture NEW SECTION

The terminology in this cheat sheet sits inside a larger landscape of data modelling schools. Naming them helps situate what we're building and recognise which industry pattern each piece is borrowing from.

Inmon — Corporate Information Factory

Bill Inmon's enterprise data warehouse model. A normalised, integrated, time-variant warehouse at the centre. Data marts derive from it for specific analytical purposes.

Strengths: coherence, single source of truth, regulatory traceability.

Trade-off: heavy upfront modelling; slow to evolve.

Where it fits in this cheat sheet: the atomic source-of-truth products (§10.5) are essentially Inmon's normalised warehouse expressed as data products. The PG conformed domain model is a domain-scoped Inmon warehouse.

Kimball — Bus Architecture

Ralph Kimball's dimensional model. The warehouse is a collection of star schemas with conformed dimensions shared across them. Bottom-up, business-process-aligned.

Strengths: consumability, performance, ease of analysis.

Trade-off: harder to enforce enterprise consistency without conformed-dimension discipline.

Where it fits in this cheat sheet: the galaxy/constellation pattern (§2) is Kimball — multiple facts sharing conformed dimensions. The grain table (§3) is straight Kimball discipline. star_dim_*, star_fact_*, obt_* naming is Kimball convention.

Data Mesh — Zhamak Dehghani

Domains own their data end-to-end. Data is a product. A federated computational governance plane provides cross-domain alignment.

Strengths: scales with org complexity; aligns ownership with knowledge.

Trade-off: requires mature engineering culture and federated governance discipline.

Where it fits in this cheat sheet: the data product framing (§4, §10.5) is Mesh's signature contribution. Treating each fact as a contracted, owned, discoverable product comes from Mesh. Without Mesh, "data product" reduces to "table with metadata."

Lakehouse — Medallion Architecture

Databricks' bronze / silver / gold pattern. Raw ingestion, conformed cleaning, consumption-optimised serving — all on the same lakehouse with a unified catalog.

Strengths: single platform for batch + streaming + ML; native support for federation.

Trade-off: requires governance discipline to keep the layers from leaking.

Where it fits in this cheat sheet: the implementation pattern. Bronze = raw ingestion. Silver = conformed atomic facts (the atomic products from §10.5). Gold = curated products and analytics models (Counterparty 360, star schemas).

The TDS Risk synthesis
What we're actually doing combines all four. Architecturally Data Mesh — domain ownership of physical data at PG level; data products as the federation primitive. Internally Inmon — within each PG, the domain model is normalised and conformed. Externally Kimball — the consumption layer (riskwh.gold) uses conformed dimensions and dimensional facts. Implemented on Lakehouse — medallion layout (bronze → silver → gold) within riskwh; app schemas as producer-owned bronze.

One-line summary: Data-mesh-shaped federation, with Kimball-style consumption layers on top of Inmon-style domain models, all running on a Lakehouse — registering up to a federated enterprise CDM.

12) Surrogate keys vs natural keys — an honest discussion NEW SECTION

One of the first decisions in dimensional modelling is whether dim_counterparty uses a surrogate key (a synthetic key invented by the warehouse, e.g., counterparty_sk = 1, 2, 3, ...) or a natural key (the identifier that already exists upstream, e.g., counterparty_id = 'LEI-XXXX' issued by an enterprise master data system).

The industry-standard answer (and why it's the standard)

Industry standard is surrogate keys.
Reason: dimensions evolve over time (counterparty hierarchies restructure, legal entities merge, parent companies acquire subsidiaries). Surrogate keys preserve historical truth across these changes via SCD Type 2 (each version of a counterparty gets its own surrogate key, while the natural key stays the same).

The case for using counterparty_id natural key directly

If counterparty_id is already created upstream by an enterprise master data system, is it stable enough to use directly? Three honest considerations:

Where natural key works

  • If counterparty_id is stable across the counterparty's lifecycle — never reassigned, never recycled, never changes when the counterparty restructures — you can use it directly.
  • For maturity-zero / Stage 1, using natural keys is genuinely faster. You skip the surrogate key dimension build, you skip the SCD Type 2 ETL, you skip teaching consumers about surrogate keys.
  • LEI (Legal Entity Identifier) is a natural key designed exactly for this — globally unique, stable, regulated, intentionally not recycled. If your counterparty_id is LEI-based, the case for natural keys is much stronger.

Where natural key breaks down

  • Mergers and acquisitions. Counterparty A acquires Counterparty B. What happens to B's counterparty_id? If it's deactivated and B's exposures are reassigned to A, you've lost the historical truth that "this exposure was originally with B." Surrogate keys with SCD Type 2 preserve this; natural keys lose it.
  • Legal entity restructures. Same parent, different legal entity ID issued. Trades booked against the old entity — do they belong to the old or new entity? With natural keys, this is ambiguous.
  • Hierarchy changes. Counterparty B was independent in 2024; became a subsidiary of A in 2025. Did B's risk roll up under A or independently in Q1 2025? The answer depends on as_of_date. Natural keys without temporal versioning can't answer this.

The honest pragmatic answer

In Stage 1 with natural keys, you make a calculated trade-off:
  • You ship faster (no surrogate key infrastructure)
  • You handle simple cases correctly (current state, current hierarchy)
  • You break or struggle on SCD-requiring cases (point-in-time historical analysis, restructuring scenarios, regulatory submissions that require historical entity state)

A middle path many banks take

Natural key with temporal snapshot — the pragmatic middle ground
Use natural keys directly and maintain an as_of_date snapshot for the dimension. So dim_counterparty is keyed on (counterparty_id, as_of_date). You don't introduce a surrogate key, but you preserve temporal versioning by snapshotting the dimension. SCD Type 2 in spirit, without the surrogate key complexity.

Industry calls this "natural key with temporal snapshot" or "Type 2 without surrogate."

The recommendation for TDS Stage 1

Start with natural keys + as_of_date snapshot.
It's faster than full surrogate keys, but doesn't lose historical truth the way pure natural keys do. If LEI is your counterparty natural key, even better — LEI is purpose-built to be stable.

If, in Stage 2-3, the complexity of historical analysis grows (e.g., regulatory back-testing, M&A scenario analysis), introduce surrogate keys then. The migration is non-trivial but doable.

This is honest. The "industry standard" of surrogate keys is the right answer for a mature, multi-year warehouse. But for maturity zero shipping in 6-9 months, surrogate keys are over-engineering. The middle path (natural key + temporal snapshot) is what most banks would actually do.

13) Other concepts a CCR cheat sheet should mention NEW SECTION

Slowly Changing Dimensions (SCD)

Counterparty hierarchy changes over time; legal entity restructures happen. The way the dimension handles change determines what historical questions can be answered.

SCD typeBehaviourUse for CCR
Type 1Overwrite — only current state is preserved.Reference data with no historical relevance (e.g., country name spelling fix).
Type 2Add new row with effective dates — full history preserved.Counterparty entity, parent hierarchy, industry classification — anything where "what was true on the trade date" matters.
Type 6Hybrid — current and historical attributes both available on the same row.Heavy reporting needs that want both "current parent" and "parent at trade date" without a join.

For CCR specifically: Type 2 (or natural key + as_of_date — see §12) is essential for counterparty and any classification used in regulatory submissions. Pure Type 1 will fail audit on historical positions.

Late-arriving facts

Exposure data for a date may arrive after the date has passed. Trade amendments correct historical bookings. The temporal mismatch matters for downstream metrics.

A trade booked on 2026-04-30 but loaded into the warehouse on 2026-05-04 needs to be associated with the dimension state on 2026-04-30, not the state on 2026-05-04. This is why dimensions need temporal versioning — and why facts need both valuation_date (when the metric is true) and load_date (when it landed in the warehouse).

Reference dataLookup values; standardised codes; rarely changes.Slow; coordinated firmwide updates.Currency codes, country codes, product type codes, industry classifications (SIC/NAICS).
Master dataCore business entities maintained centrally.Steady; SCD applies.Counterparty master, legal entity master, instrument master.
Transactional dataEvents, measurements, calculated metrics.High volume; append-only or versioned.Trades, exposures, MtM, sensitivities, breaches.
Why this distinction matters for CCR ENRICHMENT
Each type has different contract requirements:
  • Reference data contracts emphasise stability of code values; consumers can't have codes change meaning silently.
  • Master data contracts emphasise SCD policy; consumers need to know which type they're getting.
  • Transactional data contracts emphasise grain and aggregation rules; this is where the bulk of §3-§7 applies.
Mixing these up — treating reference data with the same governance posture as transactional facts, or vice versa — leads to either over-engineered ref data pipelines or under-governed transaction data.

14) Appendix — From raw data to a consumable data product NEW APPENDIX

This appendix exists because the terminology in this space is muddy. People use raw table, governed entity, curated dataset, data product, data contract, and semantic layer interchangeably — but they describe different things, often at different stages of maturity. This appendix walks through them with a concrete CCR example so the differences are unambiguous.

14.1 The most important caveat first

A data product is not necessarily one table.
This is the single most common misconception in the industry. People hear "data product" and picture a single big wide table.

A data product is a governed unit of data with a contract. That unit can be:
  • A single table (e.g., fact_exposure)
  • A small set of related tables (e.g., the Exposure product = fact_exposure + dim_netting_set + lineage views)
  • A subject area or constellation (e.g., the Counterparty Risk product = exposure + limits + collateral facts joined by conformed dimensions)
  • A view or virtual layer over physical tables
  • A streaming endpoint, an API, or a feature-store entity
The shape of a data product is whatever delivers the contracted business meaning. The contract is what makes it a product — not the table count.

14.3 Governed vs curated vs conformed — three distinct things

People use these words interchangeably. They are three distinct concepts, each describing a different aspect of the data.

TermWhat it meansWhat it implies
GovernedSubject to rules, ownership, change management, quality controls.The verb. Someone is responsible. Rules apply. Changes are managed.
CuratedDeliberately shaped, selected, refined for a purpose.The artifact characteristic. This dataset has been arranged for a use case.
ConformedAligned to a shared definition across multiple uses or contexts.Cross-context agreement. The same entity, same keys, same hierarchies wherever this data is used.
You can have governed but not curated or conformed — a raw landing zone with ownership and access controls but no shaping and no cross-context alignment. Foundation only.

You can have curated but not conformed — a Counterparty 360 view nicely shaped for credit risk, but using a counterparty definition different from how Liquidity defines counterparty. Locally good; globally inconsistent.

You can have conformed but not curated — a dim_counterparty identical across all PG uses, but the raw entity table without consumption-ready arrangement. Aligned but not yet usable for analytics out of the box.

A data product fit for analytics needs all three: conformed (so cross-product joins work), curated (so it's usable for the analytical question), and governed (so consumers can trust and depend on it).
Why each matters in CCR CONFORMED ADDED
  • Conformed is what makes dim_counterparty usable across XVALA exposure, CMOS collateral, and REGDS agreement facts. Without conformance, the joins don't work or produce wrong answers.
  • Curated is what makes the Counterparty 360 view answer George's analytics question without him doing manual work.
  • Governed is what makes consumers trust that the number won't change meaning between today and tomorrow.
The cheat sheet calls them out separately because conflating them is the source of recurring confusion in cross-team conversations.

14.4 What the data contract actually does

The contract is not documentation about the data. The contract is what converts a dataset into a product. Without a contract, you have a table. With a contract, you have a product.

Each section of the contract changes a specific aspect of the data's status:

Contract sectionWhat it changes about the data's statusWithout it...

GrainDefines the level at which numbers are mathematically valid.Aggregations silently produce wrong answers (e.g., summing PFE across netting sets).
Allowed dimensionsSays which slicings are safe.Consumers slice in ways the data doesn't support.

The contract changes context, not just metadata
Adding a contract is not "adding metadata to a table." It's asserting that the data behaves according to defined rules. The data product status is a commitment by the producer, enforced by the contract, expressed through tooling (Unity Catalog tags, Collibra registration, semantic layer rules), and consumed with confidence by the downstream user.

14.6 The progression — raw to consumption, end to end CANONICAL 6 STAGES (v10)

The data progression is six canonical stages. Each stage builds on the previous; skip a stage and the data isn't ready for what later stages assume. Authoritative and Certified are attributes a Data Product can have — they are not separate stages on this progression.

StepStageWhat it addsBrief
① Raw / LandedThe data lands, schema-typed at ingestion.Risk-generated data lands in the app schema. Schema is applied at ingestion (no JSON blobs left around). Duplicates and obvious quality issues handled in the same job. Bronze in medallion terms. Note: in older 7-stage models, this combined "Raw" and "Cleansed/Standardised" — modern lakehouse ingestion does both at once, so we collapse them here.
② ConformedIdentity resolution + shared definitions across apps.The same real-world thing — same counterparty, same trade, same currency, same time bucket — means the same thing wherever it appears. Example: XVALA records a counterparty as "ABC BANK NA"; CMOS records the same legal entity as "ABC Bank N.A."; REGDS uses internal code "X-1023". Conformance creates one canonical cp_id = 'CP000123' and maps all three back to it. Now exposure from XVALA can be joined to collateral from CMOS to regulatory positions from REGDS — and the answer is correct. Same applies to currencies, time buckets, risk factors, trade IDs. Silver layer.
③ CuratedShaped per a domain model — modelled, joined, aggregated for analytical use.This is where the domain models come in. The conformed data exists at its native grain; curation reshapes it for consumption — aggregating to a summary grain, joining facts across domains, applying business rules (breach flag = 1 if PFE > limit), choosing the dimensional model (star schema vs OBT vs 360). Curation is the act of building the actual data artifact against the model. Gold layer.
④ Data Product (Governed)Curated data + a contract.The contract embeds the semantics: grain (what one row means), aggregation rules per metric (additive / non-additive / percentile), allowed and blocked dimensions, SLA, ownership, change policy, version. Tagged in Unity Catalog; registered in Collibra. The data is now a product consumers can depend on. Authoritative and Certified are attributes a product can have — Authoritative means formally designated as canonical for a class of decisions; Certified means validated for regulated use (model inputs, regulatory submissions). These are attributes, not separate stages.
⑤ Semantic LayerRules from the contract enforced at query time.The contract specifies that PFE is non-additive across counterparties. The semantic layer is where that rule stops being documentation and becomes a runtime block: SUM(pfe) across counterparties is refused; MAX(pfe) is allowed. Implemented in dbt Metric Views, Databricks Metric Views, or BI-tool semantic models; ideally one source of truth with the BI tool mirroring it. Star schemas require a separate semantic layer — the schema itself doesn't carry the rules.
⑥ AnalyticsConsumption.Dashboards, MicroStrategy dossiers, ad-hoc exploration, regulatory feeds, model inputs. Right answers, safely, repeatably. The point of the previous five stages.
The progression is not optional — you skip stages at your peril
  • Skip ② (Conformed) → cross-product joins don't work; XVALA counterparty doesn't match CMOS counterparty; numbers are wrong silently.
  • Skip ③ (Curated) → consumers do the shaping themselves repeatedly; Counterparty 360 gets reinvented per use case; logic isn't shared.
  • Skip ④ (Data Product / Governed) → no SLA, no change policy, no accountability; pipelines break unpredictably; consumers can't depend on it.
  • Skip ⑤ (Semantic Layer) → consumers write unsafe queries; PFE gets summed; numbers are mathematically meaningless.
Each stage is a separate piece of work and delivers a separate value. The DD's role is to drive the progression — not just to deliver one stage and call it done.
Where each stage typically lives in the lakehouse MEDALLION MAPPING
  • Stage ① — Raw / Landed → Bronze layer (app schemas; CDC landings; raw drops; schema-typed at ingestion)
  • Stage ② — Conformed → Silver layer (canonical dimensions, mapping tables, identity resolution)
  • Stage ③ — Curated → Gold layer (star schemas, 360 views, OBT — modelled per the domain model)
  • Stage ④ — Data Product (Governed) → Gold + UC tags + Collibra registration (the contract wrapper around curated data)
  • Stage ⑤ — Semantic Layer → On top of Gold (dbt Metrics / Databricks Metric Views / BI-tool mirror)
  • Stage ⑥ — Analytics → Consumption layer (dashboards, BI tools, regulatory feeds)
What about Trusted / Authoritative / Certified?

In v9 these were stages ⑥ on the progression. In v10 they are attributes a Data Product (stage ④) can have, not separate stages. The reasoning:
  • Governed is binary — a dataset either has a contract or it doesn't.
  • Trusted is a cultural attribute that builds over time through use; not a milestone.
  • Authoritative is a formal organisational designation — applied to a Data Product.
  • Certified is a validation attribute (often regulatory or model-risk) — also applied to a Data Product.
A new Data Product is governed on day 1. It becomes trusted after months of stable use. It becomes authoritative when a governance body formally designates it. It becomes certified after model risk validates it for regulated use. None of these are stages in the sense of "data flowing forward" — they are properties accumulating on the same artifact.

14.7 The value-add summary — three independent things

When someone asks "What does a data product give me that a raw table doesn't?" — the honest answer is three things, each of which is independent of the others:

1. Reusability

A canned query solves one question. A data product is a building block. Build it once, answer many questions.

Without: every new question reinvents the data wrangling.

2. Trust and stability

The contract commits to grain, schema stability, change-management notice, quality checks. Consumers can build on top safely.

Without: pipelines break silently. No one to ask. No SLA.

3. Safe self-service

The semantic layer enforces grain and aggregation rules. Wrong queries become impossible. BI users get drag-and-drop with guardrails.

Without: business users write unsafe queries. PFE gets summed. Numbers are wrong, presented as truth.

Three independent value-adds — and the cost of conflating them
A "data product" that gives you only #1 (reusability) without #2 (trust) is just a shared view. A product that gives #1 + #2 without #3 (semantic enforcement) leaves consumers free to make grain mistakes. A product that gives all three is what consumers actually need — and it requires all three workstreams (governance, contracts, semantic layer) to deliver.

Many things called "data products" in industry deliver only one of these three. When designing the TDS Risk data product portfolio, be explicit about which value-adds each product delivers — and which still need work.

15.3 The wide-table fallacy — why "one big CP_360 table" doesn't work

The natural temptation
The first instinct is: "let's build one wide table — Counterparty 360 — that has everything George wants in it. One row per counterparty. Easy to query, easy to dashboard."

This doesn't work. Here's why, in concrete terms.

If you tried to build a single CP_360 wide table, you'd have to choose one grain for the table. Three options exist; all are bad.

Choice of grainWhat happensWhy it fails
Trade-level
(trade + valuation_date)		Each row is a trade. Exposure metrics (which live at netting-set grain) get duplicated across every trade in the netting set. Collateral (which lives at agreement grain) gets duplicated across every trade in the agreement.Summing PFE across the table multiplies it by the number of trades per netting set. Summing collateral multiplies it by the number of trades per agreement. The numbers are wrong — silently and badly.
Netting-set-level
(counterparty + netting_set + valuation_date)		Each row is a netting set. Loses trade-level detail. Sensitivities (per trade, per risk factor) can no longer be represented. CS01 can no longer be sliced by tenor or risk factor.George loses the trade and sensitivity drill-down he explicitly asked for. The "see trades for ABC Bank" experience is broken.
Counterparty-level
(counterparty + valuation_date)		Each row is one counterparty per date. Loses netting-set, trade, sensitivity, scenario detail entirely. Just a summary.The "summary" is genuinely useful (this is Layer 1, in fact) — but it isn't a 360. It's just a summary, with no drill-down. George can see "ABC Bank, total PFE = $40M" but can't answer "which netting set, which scenario, which trade is driving that?"
The conclusion
There is no single grain that supports George's full ask. Therefore there is no single wide table that can be CP_360. The "360" is necessarily a layered architecture, not a wide table.

15.5 The user experience that delivers George's ask

George opens the dossier, types ABC Bank. The BI tool does this:

  1. Loads Layer 1 instantly. One row from cp_risk_summary populates the top of the dossier — name, profile, headline metrics, breach status, trend sparklines.
  2. Renders tabs. Trades / Exposure / Sensitivities / Collateral / Stress / Limits / CVA. Each tab is initially empty (lazy-loaded).
  3. User clicks Trades tab. BI loads fact_trade WHERE counterparty_id = 'ABC' — gets ~500 trade rows. Shows them in a grid with MtM, notional, product, underlying.
  4. User clicks Exposure tab. BI loads fact_exposure WHERE counterparty_id = 'ABC' — gets ~200 rows (netting-set × time-bucket × scenario). Shows EE/PFE chart and table.
  5. User clicks Sensitivities tab. BI loads fact_sensitivities WHERE counterparty_id = 'ABC' — gets ~5,000 rows. Pre-aggregates by risk_factor + tenor in the chart; allows drill to trade level on demand.
  6. User clicks Collateral tab. BI loads fact_collateral WHERE counterparty_id = 'ABC' — gets ~10 rows. Shows IA/VM/IM split by agreement.
  7. User clicks Stress tab. BI loads fact_stress WHERE counterparty_id = 'ABC' — gets ~300 rows (scenario × netting-set × bucket). Shows stressed exposure under each scenario.
  8. User clicks Limits tab. BI loads fact_limits WHERE counterparty_id = 'ABC' — gets ~5 rows. Shows limits and current utilisation.
Why this works KEY POINT
  • Each tab loads only what the user asks for — no Cartesian explosion.
  • Each tab respects the natural grain of its source — no wrong aggregations.
  • The user perceives one unified "ABC Bank" experience — the orchestration is the magic.
  • The summary loads in milliseconds; the detail loads only when needed.
  • Each Layer 2 product can be developed, governed, and SLA'd independently.

15.6 Specific worked grains and aggregations

Three concrete examples of how the semantic layer enforces correct behaviour across the layered architecture:

Example A — Total PFE for ABC Bank

From Layer 1: SELECT pfe FROM cp_risk_summary WHERE counterparty_id = 'ABC' AND valuation_date = '2026-05-04'; returns one number — the pre-baked MAX across time buckets and netting sets, computed according to the documented rule. Fast, simple, contracted.

From Layer 2: If the user wants to see PFE by netting set or by time bucket, the Exposure tab queries fact_exposure at native grain and the semantic layer enforces the rules — for instance, refusing to sum PFE across netting sets without approved allocation.

Example B — Collateral by type for ABC Bank

From Layer 1: Headline shows total_ia, total_vm, total_im separately. The contract makes the type distinction explicit; never combined into a single "collateral" number.

From Layer 2: Collateral tab queries fact_collateral showing breakdown by agreement and type. Aggregations done in the tab respect the type distinction.

Example C — CS01 by tenor for ABC Bank

From Layer 1: Total CS01 (summed across trades and tenors) appears as a headline number.

From Layer 2: Sensitivities tab queries fact_sensitivities. Chart aggregates SUM(CS01) by tenor — semantic layer permits this because CS01 is additive. Tab also supports drill to trade-and-risk-factor level.

15.7 Where the cheat-sheet concepts land in this example

Cheat-sheet conceptWhere it shows up in George's ask
Grain (§3)The grain mapping table in §15.2 is the foundation. Without it, the wide-table fallacy is inevitable.
Galaxy schema (§2)The seven Layer 2 atomic products together form a galaxy — multiple facts sharing conformed dimensions (counterparty, netting_set, agreement, valuation_date).
Conformed dimensions (§14.3)For drill-down to work, every Layer 2 product must agree on what counterparty_id means, what netting_set_id means, what agreement_id means.
Three-way data product taxonomy (§4)cp_risk_summary is curated/360. The seven Layer 2 products are atomic primary. There may also be a regulatory/operational version (e.g., daily SA-CCR feed) — a third type.
Data contracts (§5)Each of the eight products needs a contract. The summary's contract documents which aggregations are pre-baked (e.g., "PFE = MAX across time buckets, scenarios using max envelope rule").
Semantic layer (§6)Enforces aggregation rules within each tab. Refuses unsafe operations (e.g., summing PFE across netting sets without allocation).
Safe vs unsafe queries (§10.2 vs §10.3)The aggregate-then-combine pattern from §10.3 is exactly what each tab's queries do — never row-joining facts at different grains.
Bottom line for George SUMMARY
George's "show me ABC Bank" ask cannot be served by a single wide table. It requires:
  • One curated summary data product at counterparty grain (Layer 1)
  • Seven atomic data products at their native grains (Layer 2)
  • A BI orchestration layer that ties them together as a single user experience (Layer 3)
This is the architecturally correct pattern. It also happens to be how every counterparty risk dashboard at every major bank is built. The summary loads instantly; the drill-down is on demand; the grain is preserved end to end.
