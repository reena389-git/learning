Foundational Risk Analytics Learning Layer

A simple, connected learning document for explaining analytics concepts before going into deeper CCR implementation.

1. Purpose

This document explains modern analytics concepts using a simplified risk example. It is not meant to teach complex quantitative finance. It is meant to help readers understand why analytics needs grain, data products, semantic rules, and governed self-service.

Teaching sequence:
Economic intuition → analytics intuition → architecture realization.

2. The Simple Business Story

Imagine a bank trades with a counterparty called ABC Bank. If markets move, the value of those trades can change. If the trade becomes valuable to us, ABC Bank may owe us money. That amount is called exposure.

CounterpartyTrade Value TodayPlain-English Meaning
ABC Bank$2MIf ABC Bank defaulted today, we may have $2M at risk.
Intuition: Exposure is not just a number. It is a measure of how much we could lose if the counterparty fails to pay us.

3. Exposure Changes Through Time

Markets move every day. Interest rates, FX rates, credit spreads, and volatility can all change. So exposure can become larger or smaller in the future.

CounterpartyFuture TimeExposure
ABC BankToday$2M
ABC Bank1 Year$4M
ABC Bank3 Years$1M
ABC Bank5 Years$0.5M
This time-based view is called an exposure profile.

4. First Analytics Concept: Grain

Grain means: what one row represents.

DatasetOne Row RepresentsGrain
Exposure profileExposure for one counterparty at one future time pointcounterparty + future time
Sensitivity datasetSensitivity for one counterparty to one risk factorcounterparty + risk factor
If you do not understand grain, you can write a query that looks correct but gives the wrong number.

5. A Different Dataset: Sensitivities

Now imagine the business asks: what causes exposure to move?

The answer is: risk factors.

CounterpartyRisk FactorSensitivity
ABC BankInterest Rates$40K
ABC BankCredit Spreads$80K
ABC BankFX$15K

One row represents sensitivity for one counterparty to one risk factor.

6. Why Analytics Breaks: The Bad Join

Exposure Dataset

CounterpartyFuture TimeExposure
ABC Bank1 Year$4M

Sensitivity Dataset

CounterpartyRisk FactorSensitivity
ABC BankInterest Rates$40K
ABC BankCredit Spreads$80K

Incorrect Direct Join

CounterpartyFuture TimeExposureRisk Factor
ABC Bank1 Year$4MInterest Rates
ABC Bank1 Year$4MCredit Spreads
The exposure row has been duplicated. If the user sums exposure, the answer becomes $8M instead of $4M.
Correct rule: Aggregate each dataset at the right grain first, then combine.

7. Additive vs Non-Additive Metrics

Metric TypeCan It Be Summed?Example
AdditiveYesSales amount, quantity, current exposure
Non-additiveNo, or only under controlled rulesAverages, percentages, percentiles, PFE

Why PFE Is Non-Additive

PFE means Potential Future Exposure. It answers: “How large could exposure become in a bad but plausible future?”

CounterpartyPFEMeaning
ABC Bank$9MHigh-percentile future exposure estimate
XYZ Fund$7MHigh-percentile future exposure estimate
PFE is a percentile-style risk estimate. Percentiles cannot be casually added like cash balances because the underlying future exposure distributions may overlap, offset, or behave differently under market scenarios.
Better wording: PFE is non-additive and requires controlled aggregation rules. Sometimes a curated metric may use MAX, sometimes a risk engine recomputation is required, and sometimes the metric must remain at its original grain.

8. What Is a Data Product?

A data product is a trusted reusable analytical dataset with rules, ownership, and intended use.

The product already applies approved calculations, standardized identifiers, trusted dimensions, and governed aggregation logic.

9. What Is a Semantic Layer?

A semantic layer is a safety layer between raw data and business questions.

Without Semantic Layer

  • Every team writes SQL differently
  • Filters differ
  • Aggregation differs
  • Dashboards conflict
  • Users reconcile manually

With Semantic Layer

  • Official metrics are named
  • Aggregation rules are applied
  • Unsafe slicing is blocked or warned
  • Users self-serve safely
  • Numbers reconcile

Example business question:

Show official exposure by region.

The semantic layer applies the approved calculation, filters, trusted dimensions, and aggregation rules.

10. Data Product with Built-In Semantics vs Star Schema with Semantics to Implement

This is an important distinction.

A. Data Product with Built-In Semantics

The product is already packaged for a business use case.

  • Metric names are business-friendly
  • Default filters are applied
  • Aggregation rules are embedded
  • Unsafe detail is hidden or separated
  • Consumers use it directly
CounterpartyOfficial ExposureOfficial PFE
ABC Bank$4M$9M

Best for: common business questions, dashboards, executive views, governed consumption.

B. Star Schema with Semantics to Implement

The star schema provides flexible analytical structure, but rules still need to be implemented in a semantic or BI layer.

  • Facts and dimensions are available
  • Users can slice flexibly
  • Metric behavior must be defined
  • BI tool must enforce levels and aggregation
  • More powerful, but easier to misuse
fact_exposure joins dim_counterparty joins dim_time_bucket joins dim_scenario

Best for: flexible exploration, governed self-service, reusable analytical modeling.

Practical Difference

QuestionData Product AnswerStar Schema Answer
Who defines PFE behavior?Already packaged in productDefined in semantic / BI layer
Can user misuse grain?Less likelyPossible unless guarded
FlexibilityLowerHigher
Governance burdenProduct ownerSemantic / BI model owner
A good architecture usually needs both: curated data products for common business questions and star schemas for controlled analytical exploration.

11. Why Wide Tables Fail

Many organizations try to create one giant table with everything in it. This often fails because different measures live at different grains.

MetricNatural Grain
Exposurecounterparty + future time
Sensitivitiescounterparty + risk factor
Stress exposurecounterparty + stress scenario + future time
Limitscounterparty + facility
One giant table forces incompatible grains together. That creates duplicated rows, broken aggregation, and unclear ownership.

Better pattern:

Atomic products → governed dimensions → semantic layer → trusted analytics

12. Final Mental Model

Applications generate calculations. Analytics products industrialize meaning. Star schemas organize facts and dimensions. Semantic layers enforce safe usage. Business users consume trusted reusable insights.
The goal is not simply more dashboards. The goal is trusted, reusable analytics that scales across the organization.
