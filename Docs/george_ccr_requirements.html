Context — what the dashboard is and isn't

The CCR_Analytics_Report in PowerBI is a visualization shell over pre-computed SCREAM reports. SCREAM (the Credit Risk System, "CRS") produces canned reports with all aggregations, limit roll-ups, utilisations, exposure metrics, and breach flags already calculated. PowerBI does not aggregate, join, or recompute — it renders what SCREAM hands it. Every limitation surfaced in this document traces back to that single architectural fact.

ReferenceThe Legal Name View page in the CCR_Analytics_Report — the central daily working view. Left rail: 20+ pages. Top: CPTY filter (counterparty wildcard search), Limit & Utilisation chart, Current Exposure mini-chart. Middle: CCR Line Details. Lower: Trade Details and Party Details panels. Most other pages in the dashboard are variants or specialisations of this core layout.

The dashboard has 20+ tabs:

  • Counterparty exposure views (Legal Name View, ULT_Parent View, TDLegal Entity View, Clients Legal Name View)
  • Country-of-risk views (CountryOfRisk View, Hist Treasury Exposure)
  • Jurisdictional monitoring (ISRAELI_Notional_Monitoring)
  • FX/PnL daily delta attribution (FXNotionalDeltaPnL_G, FXNotionalDeltaPnL_S)
  • Product-specific views (REPO_Client_Mapping, IR/CCS Term TDBK, Hedge Fund Manager Portfolio, Haircut Adj Report, HFund TopView Limit, TOP 20 NIG Exposures)
  • Regulatory (CAIRO 2 - Facility Exposure, CAIRO 4 - FI Peer Comparison)
  • Operational (FilterOfReportLines, SampleDayLightReport, ExceededONLY View, REPO_Client_Mapping, Data Check)

George opened the session by acknowledging that the build has materially helped the GMC team — work that previously took hours of manual assembly is now visualised. His framing throughout was constructive: "this works, here's what's still painful, here's what I wish it could do."

Section 1 — Explicit requirements

R1 — Decompose into dimensions; filter across multiple dimensions to narrow and widen

R1The dashboard must support tabbed dimensional navigation — narrow scope by chaining dimension selections, widen by removing them.

What he showed

Today the dashboard has a flat list of 20+ pages on the left rail. Each page has its own filter chrome. Switching analytical context (e.g. from "exposure by counterparty" to "exposure by region then counterparty") means opening a different page or hand-applying filters. The report layout is fixed; the user can only operate within whatever slicing the page designer chose.

What he asked for

Tabs across the top of the dashboard representing dimensions:

CounterpartyCountry of RiskRegionDeskBooking EntityIndustryProductLine TypeTenor Bucket

Click a tab to narrow the working scope, then click the next tab to narrow further. Example he gave: "It is Central America, then counterparty there" — i.e. select region first, then drill into counterparties within that region. The same mechanism widens — un-select a dimension to broaden the view back out.

The litmus-test question

George asked: "Show me all my contingent lines that are running hot in the 1-2 year bucket regardless of counterparty." Today this requires a different SCREAM report. In a dimensional model it is a one-line filter — Line Type = CONT, Tenor Bucket = 1-2Y, sort by utilisation. Resolving this question is the test that the refactor is complete.

Data needs
Conformed dimension tables (Silver layer)
  • dim_counterparty — with parent / agent / ultimate parent hierarchy, internal CP_CODE, legal name, Bloomberg ticker, INTERNAL flag
  • dim_country — country code, country of risk classification, regulatory regime tags
  • dim_region — region code, region grouping, market close timing
  • dim_industry — NAICS / GICS / TDS internal industry taxonomy
  • dim_desk — desk code, desk name, asset class, location
  • dim_booking_entity — entity code (TDBK, TDGF, TDEU, TDSC, TDSE, TDSU, TBNA, TCAX), entity name, jurisdiction
  • dim_product — proper product taxonomy: Equity Swap, Vanilla IRS, FX Forward, CAP, Asian Commodity Option, Repo, etc.
  • dim_line — line ID, line type (CONT, C2C, committed/uncommitted, daylight), line purpose (CP, HC), owning entity, governing policy, product scope
  • dim_tenor_bucket — bucket ID, label (N-MTM, 0-3M, 3M-1Y, 1-2Y, 2-5Y, 5-10Y, 10-50Y), maturity boundaries
  • dim_currency — currency code, reporting flag (USD/CAD primary)

R2 — Decompose exposure properly: limits, MTM, direct, indirect, default risk

R2Exposure must be surfaced as five distinct concepts, simultaneously, joined to whichever counterparty / country / dimension is in scope.

What he showed

The Current Exposure bar chart on the upper right of Legal Name View shows one number per currency (e.g. RBC: ~2,116M USD). This is the limit utilisation — how much of TDS's approved credit capacity is being consumed.

Current stateThe Current Exposure mini-chart on the upper right shows limit utilisation only — one bar per currency. No MTM, no collateral mitigant, no direct/indirect split, no default risk number.
What he asked for

Exposure decomposed into five concepts:

ConceptWhat it answers
LimitsHow much approved credit capacity does TDS have for this counterparty? Broken by line, by tenor bucket, by currency.
MTM (current exposure)What's TDS's economic exposure to this counterparty right now — replacement cost if they default today?
Direct exposureExposure arising from trades TDS has with this counterparty.
Indirect exposureExposure to this counterparty even when not the direct trading party — as collateral provider, security issuer, parent / guarantor, related entity, agent. (NVIDIA example: not a counterparty, but appears as collateral.)
Default riskIf the counterparty actually defaults, what does TDS actually lose? Gross MTM minus collateral held (with haircuts), plus PFE add-on.

The full stack:

Limit (capacity)5,000M USD
↳ Limit Utilisation2,116M USD ← what shows on the chart today

Direct MTM exposure142M USD (Type 2 — economic, not capacity)
+ Indirect exposure28M USD (NVIDIA-style — collateral / issuer paths)
= Total Gross Exposure170M USD
− Collateral held(98M) USD (IM + VM, haircut-adjusted)
= Net Exposure72M USD
+ PFE add-on12M USD (potential future exposure)
= Default Risk Exposure84M USD ← what TDS actually loses on default

Today the dashboard shows row 2 only (limit utilisation). George wants all rows surfaced. This is the most consequential ask in the session because it reshapes the headline measure of CCR.

Data needs
Fact tables required (Silver/Gold layer)
  • fact_limit — line_id, value_date, tenor_bucket, currency, approved_limit. Source: SCREAM line tables.
  • fact_limit_utilization — line_id, value_date, tenor_bucket, currency, utilisation, availability, max_usage. Source: SCREAM utilisation tables.
  • fact_mtm — trade_id, value_date, mtm_value, mtm_currency. Source: valuation engines (RCAPS, XVALA), or upstream booking systems (CALYPSO, MUREX, SOPHIS).
  • fact_collateral — counterparty_id, agreement_id, value_date, collateral_type (IM/VM), asset_id (the actual security/cash posted), gross_value, haircut_pct, net_value. Source: CMOS / Colline.
  • fact_pfe — counterparty_id or netting_set_id, value_date, horizon, confidence_level, pfe_value (standard and stressed). Source: RCS.
  • fact_indirect_exposure — derived view joining collateral + securities reference + relationship hierarchy to identify all paths from a non-trading party to TDS exposure.
Computation required
  • Default Risk = SUM(MTM) - SUM(Collateral × (1 - Haircut)) + PFE_add_on. Computed in Gold layer / analytics view.
  • Direct vs Indirect classification on the indirect exposure view: must distinguish whether the searched party is the trading counterparty (direct) or appears via another path (indirect: collateral provider, issuer, parent, related, agent).

R3 — Counterparty wildcard search must extend to indirect parties

R3The CPTY search must surface any party that creates exposure to TDS — direct trading counterparties AND indirect parties (collateral providers, security issuers, parents/guarantors).

What he showed

The CPTY filter on the left of Legal Name View is a wildcard search drilling counterparty legal name down to credit lines. Today it returns only entities that appear in SCREAM's counterparty list — i.e. direct trading counterparties.

Current stateSearching "royal b" returns four trading-counterparty matches under SCREAM's known list. There is no way to also surface, e.g., parties that appear only as collateral providers under CSAs.
What he asked for

The search must extend to:

  • Collateral providers — any party whose collateral TDS holds, even if TDS does not trade with them. The NVIDIA example: NVIDIA is not a TDS counterparty. NVIDIA appears as collateral posted under various CSA agreements with hedge funds. Searching "NVIDIA" today returns nothing; it must return the indirect-exposure view.
  • Indirect exposure paths for a known counterparty — when he searches JPMC, today he sees direct exposure (TDS↔JPMC trades). He also wants indirect JPMC exposure surfaced: where JPMC has posted collateral, where JPMC is the issuer of securities held as collateral, where JPMC is parent/guarantor of an entity TDS trades with.

This requirement is tightly coupled to R2 — you cannot show indirect exposure without first being able to search for indirect parties.

Data needs
Tables required
  • dim_party (extends dim_counterparty) — universe of all parties that could have any role: trading counterparty, collateral provider, security issuer, guarantor. Single search-able party master.
  • dim_security — for any security held as collateral or referenced in a trade: ticker, ISIN, CUSIP, issuer_party_id, asset class, country.
  • dim_party_relationship — links between parties: parent_party_id, child_party_id, relationship_type (parent, subsidiary, guarantor, agent, related_entity), effective_dates.
  • fact_collateral — must carry both posting_party_id (who posted) AND asset_id (the security or cash posted) — so the issuer can be derived via dim_security.issuer_party_id.
  • A Gold-layer view v_party_exposure_paths that for any party_id returns: direct trades, collateral postings (as provider), securities held (as issuer), related-party exposure (via relationship table).

R5 — Flexible, user-selectable columns

R5Column visibility must adapt to context — either automatically based on what's in scope, or via a user-controlled column picker.

What he showed

The Trade Details panel has a fixed column set — Value Date, Book, C Line, Counterparty Code, Deal ID, Deal Type, Desk, Trade Date, Maturity Date, Source System, Version, Years To Maturity, Deal M2M Currency, Deal M2M, Principal 1, Principal 2, C2C Charge, etc. These columns were modeled for OTC derivatives.

The complaint

When SFT trades, manual adjustments, IM postings, or other non-derivative bookings flow through, the data lands in semantically wrong columns — or the right columns are missing entirely.

What he asked for

Two complementary mechanisms:

  • Context-sensitivity: column set adapts based on the trade types in scope. Derivatives in scope → derivative columns surface. SFT in scope → SFT columns surface. IM in scope → collateral-relevant columns surface.
  • Column picker: a side panel (right-hand side) where the user explicitly picks columns to show / hide. Saved as user preference.
Data needs
Model required
  • The trade fact must be designed wide enough to carry asset-class-specific attributes — or alternatively, a normalized model where common attributes sit on fact_trade and asset-class-specific attributes sit on satellite tables (fact_trade_derivative_attrs, fact_trade_sft_attrs, fact_trade_collateral_attrs) joined via trade_id.
  • UI metadata: per-asset-class column relevance config — which columns to show by default, which to suppress.

R6 — IM / Adjustment / Deal Type conformance

R6"IM," "Adjustment," and similar values must not appear in the Deal Type column. Product, booking purpose, and lifecycle event are three different concepts and require three different fields.

What he showed

On the Alyeska Master Fund row, the Trade Details panel includes an entry where Deal Type = IM, sitting alongside Deal Type = Equity Swap, CAP, Asian Commodity Option, Commodity Forward.

Current state — data conformance defectAlyeska Trade Details. Multiple rows show Deal Type = Equity Swap (correctly representing an instrument). The bottom row shows Deal Type = IM with Book = TCIM_IMBOOK, Deal ID = USDCASH00006D1, Desk = IMDESK. The row identifies itself as IM-related four different ways and never identifies the underlying product it collateralises.
His specific question

"Why is IM appearing under Deal Type? Should the underlying product not be there instead?"

Why this matters

IM is not a financial instrument. IM is a booking purpose — the row exists to represent an Initial Margin posting against an underlying portfolio. Equity Swap is a product. Putting both in the same column conflates two different conceptual axes. The Deal Type column is being asked to hold three different kinds of values:

ValueWhat it actually classifiesWhere it should live
Equity Swap, Vanilla IRS, FX Forward, CAP, OptionProduct / instrument typedim_product
IM, VMBooking purposedim_trade_purpose / dim_collateral_type
Adjustment, Reversal, NovationLifecycle eventdim_lifecycle_event

The same critique applies to "Adjustment" appearing as a Deal Type for manual booking corrections (visible on the Zymer rows: MANUAL_ASG, MANUAL_GED TDBLN EUCEDOPT, MANUAL_LME-AH). Adjustment is a lifecycle action, not an instrument. The underlying product is being lost.

What "IM" should look like properly modelled
FieldToday (flat)Tomorrow (dimensional)
Deal TypeIMCash Collateral
Trade Purpose(none)Initial Margin
Collateral Type(none)IM
Underlying Portfolio(none — lost)ALYA_C2C Equity Swap Book
Data needs
Conformed dimensions required
  • dim_product — instrument types only (Equity Swap, IRS, FX Forward, Cash Collateral, etc.)
  • dim_trade_purpose — Risk-Taking, Hedge, Initial Margin, Variation Margin, Adjustment-Reversal
  • dim_collateral_type — IM, VM, fee, premium
  • dim_lifecycle_event — New, Active, Amended, Cancelled, Adjusted, Novated, Reversed, Expired
Fact table changes
  • fact_trade — must carry FKs to all four dimensions above. The trade can simultaneously be (Equity Swap, Risk-Taking, New) or (Cash Collateral, Initial Margin, New). Today these distinctions are squashed.
  • fact_trade — must carry underlying_portfolio_id for IM rows so they link back to the trades they collateralise.

R7 — Risk movement attribution across all asset classes

R7The dashboard must show "where today's risk lives" across all asset classes — not just FX. The same six-panel attribution paradigm visible on the FX page must extend to Equities, Rates, Credit, and Commodities.

George's underlying principle

"Even though the OTC market is big, it's not really the volume that is important at times. It's really important to see where the risk lies. There's high volatility for FX rates or for equities. We need to be able to get that view to see which asset types or where is that high volatility being felt — and being able to drill down on it."

In credit risk, volume is not risk. A counterparty with 10,000 trades may not be material today; a counterparty with 5 large positions in a moving asset class may dominate today's PnL. The dashboard should surface the latter, not the former.

What he showed (the working FX prototype)

The FXNotionalDeltaPnL_G page demonstrates exactly this approach — but only for FX.

Reference / aspirationalThe FX risk attribution page. Six panels: (1) Entity View by booking entity with Long/Short/Net/Gross MTM Impact; (2) Top 10 PnL Movement waterfall; (3) FX Rate heat map by currency × time horizon (DoD/W1W/W2W/MoM/QoQ); (4) Top counterparty contributors with NetImpact in dollars; (5) CCY decomposition by entity; (6) CCY pair view. Slicers: NIG/IG and PnL Impact close region (London) vs Last close region (Toronto).
What he asked for — generalise the paradigm

The same six-panel structure, parameterised by asset class:

Asset classSensitivity typesMarket moves to watch
FX ✓ todayFX Delta, FX VegaCCY pair % moves; FX vol surfaces
EquitiesEquity Delta, Equity Vega, Dividend sensitivityIndex level moves (S&P, NDX, TSX, NIKKEI, FTSE); single-stock prices; equity vol surfaces
RatesDV01, Key-rate DV01s, Rate Vega, ConvexityYield curve shifts by tenor and currency; basis swap spreads; swaption vol surfaces
CreditCS01, Jump-to-Default, Credit VegaSingle-name CDS spreads; index spreads (CDX IG/HY, iTraxx Main/Crossover); credit vol
CommoditiesCommodity Delta, Commodity Vega, Calendar spread sensitivitySpot prices (oil, gas, metals, ags); forward curves; commodity vol

Volatility is not a separate asset class — vega appears within each. The five real asset classes are FX, Equities, Rates, Credit, Commodities.

Why credit movement is especially important to highlight

Credit is where market-implied risk and counterparty risk converge. If a counterparty's own CDS spread widens significantly, that's the market signalling perceived deterioration of its creditworthiness. A counterparty whose CDS spread doubles in a week may be heading toward distress — even if internal rating hasn't been downgraded. So Credit attribution gives two outputs: PnL impact from spread moves, AND a counterparty deterioration early-warning list.

Data needs
New fact / dim required — these are NEW data domains the analytics layer doesn't have today
  • fact_sensitivities — trade_id, valuation_date, market_factor_id, sensitivity_type (delta, dv01, cs01, vega, etc.), sensitivity_value, currency. One row per trade per market factor per sensitivity type. Source: valuation engines (RCAPS, XVALA), or pre-computed in source systems (CALYPSO, MUREX, SOPHIS). Refreshed daily; possibly intraday for fast-moving books.
  • dim_market_factor — factor_id, factor_type (FX rate / equity index / single stock / yield curve point / CDS spread / commodity spot / implied vol point), asset_class (FX/Equity/Rates/Credit/Commodity), factor_description, currency, region.
  • fact_market_data — factor_id, date, eod_value, intraday_value, change_dod, change_wow, change_mom, change_qoq. Source: Bloomberg, Refinitiv, internal market data hub.
Computation required (Gold layer)
  • v_pnl_attribution — joins fact_sensitivities × fact_market_data, computes pnl_impact = sensitivity × market_move per trade per factor, aggregates to counterparty / desk / book / asset class.
  • Concentration metrics — for any aggregation level, what % of impact is concentrated in the top 1, top 5, top 10 contributors.

Section 2 — Gaps in the current dashboard

Organised by root cause. Some gaps are issues George raised explicitly; others became apparent walking through the screens.

2.1 Gaps rooted in SCREAM's report structure

G1 — The dashboard is rigid because SCREAM is rigid.Root cause

Every limitation traces back to one fact: PowerBI is rendering pre-computed SCREAM reports without re-shaping them. Aggregations are done. Roll-ups are done. The data arrives flat and pre-joined. PowerBI cannot offer flexibility the source data does not contain. "These dashboards are simply depicting whatever data was in those reports. So it is pretty rigid."

G2 — Exposure shown as one number — no decomposition.

The Current Exposure bar chart shows limit utilisation only. There is no separation of MTM vs default risk, no surfacing of collateral as a mitigant, no direct vs indirect path, no PFE add-on. The headline measure of CCR is collapsed to a single dimension when it has at least five.

G3 — Deal Type column conflates three different conceptual axes.

Product types (Equity Swap, CAP, Forward), booking purposes (IM), and lifecycle events (Adjustment) all appear in the same column. By collapsing them, the dashboard cannot answer questions like "show me all my equity-swap-related exposure including the IM that backs it" or "show me all my IM postings broken down by underlying product."

G4 — Trade Details columns are derivatives-shaped — non-derivative bookings land mis-aligned.

When SFT trades, manual adjustments, IM bookings, or other non-derivative records flow through, attributes land in mismatched columns. Manual adjustment rows (MANUAL_ASG, MANUAL_GED TDBLN EUCEDOPT, MANUAL_LME-AH) lose the underlying product. IM rows say "IM" four ways and identify nothing about what they collateralise. SFT trade economics don't fit the derivative column structure.

2.2 Gaps rooted in missing data integration

G5 — No CMOS / Colline integration — collateral lives in email.

The daily margin call digest is an Outlook email. Margin call counts, gross margin call amounts vs DXY index, expected vs actual net margin — all of this exists as static charts pasted into a daily distribution. It is not queryable, drillable, or joinable to the SCREAM exposure data sitting one tab away. Without this join, default risk cannot be computed (R2).

G6 — No way to search non-trading counterparties (collateral providers, issuers).

The CPTY filter is sourced from SCREAM's counterparty list — only direct trading counterparties. Parties that appear as collateral providers (NVIDIA), security issuers, related entities (parent / guarantor), or agents are not searchable. There is no indirect-exposure surface.

G7 — Industry classification field present but unpopulated.

The Party Details panel has an INDUSTRY_RATING column. On most rows captured in the screens it is blank. Industry classification is one of George's original 12 requirements and a critical concentration-risk dimension. The column exists; the data does not.

G8 — No sensitivities data joined into the analytics view.

The FX page demonstrates that risk attribution is possible — but only because FX sensitivities (FX deltas) appear to be available in some form. Equity, rates, credit, and commodity sensitivities are not surfaced. Without sensitivities, risk movement attribution across asset classes is not possible.

G9 — No conformed market data domain.

FX rate moves are visible in the FX page heat map. Equity index moves, yield curve shifts, CDS spread changes, and commodity price moves are not in the dashboard at all. Without market data as a conformed dimension joined to sensitivities, the recipe for risk movement attribution cannot be applied.

2.3 Gaps rooted in page-per-view design

G10 — Hard-coded country pages instead of a country-driven view.

The Israeli Notional Monitoring page is the canonical example. Israel got its own bespoke page rather than being folded into the parameterised structure already demonstrated by Hist Treasury Exposure. This will not scale to other regulatory regimes.

G11 — Flat 20+ tab navigation — no dimensional composition.

Today the user picks a page, then applies filters within it. There is no way to compose a view by chaining dimension selections (Region → Country → Counterparty → Desk). Every distinct slice requires either a different page or hand-managing multiple slicers.

G12 — No user control over column visibility.

The trade and party detail tables expose every column, every time. There is no column picker, no context-sensitivity, no user-saved column preferences.

2.4 Architectural gap

G13 — Static, point-in-time. No re-aggregation, no re-computation, no cross-source joins.

Because SCREAM produces canned reports with all calculations baked in, the dashboard cannot:

  • Re-aggregate to a level not pre-computed by SCREAM (e.g. roll up by industry sector if SCREAM didn't compute that)
  • Apply user-defined filters that change the aggregation grain
  • Compute period comparisons (DoD, WoW, MoM) that SCREAM didn't pre-stage
  • Join to data outside SCREAM's report scope (CMOS, market data, sensitivities, party reference data)
  • Answer cross-counterparty questions like "all my contingent lines running hot in the 1-2yr bucket"

Every "more dynamic" thing George wants requires moving from report consumption to model consumption.

Forward-looking improvements — what the next version of the dashboard should do, beyond fixing the current gaps. Many of these naturally fall out of the data refactor described in Section 4.

E1 — Tabbed dimensional composition at the top of the dashboard.

Top-of-page tabs: CounterpartyCountryRegionDeskBooking EntityIndustryProductLine TypeTenor — user clicks a tab to enter a dimension, selects a value, working scope narrows. Subsequent tabs operate within that narrowed scope. Un-select to widen.

Section 4 — The refactor: from canned reports to dimensional model

The arc of George's session is: the BI tool is doing fine; the limitations are in the data shape it's being given. Realising the enrichments above requires refactoring from report-fed dashboard to model-fed analytics layer.

The core principle

Today: SCREAM produces flat reports → PowerBI renders them. Tomorrow: SCREAM (and Colline, CMOS, valuation engines, market data, reference data) feed a conformed Silver layer in Databricks → an analytics view (Gold) joins facts to dimensions → BI tool (PowerBI, Strategy, anything) queries dimensionally. The BI tool is interchangeable; the model is the asset.

4.1 The four-layer architecture

Layer 1 — Conformed dimensions The "who and what." dim_party (with parent / agent / ultimate parent hierarchy, internal/external attributes), dim_country, dim_industry, dim_region, dim_desk, dim_booking_entity, dim_currency, dim_tenor_bucket, dim_product, dim_trade_purpose, dim_lifecycle_event, dim_collateral_type, dim_agreement, dim_line, dim_security, dim_market_factor, dim_party_relationship.
Layer 2 — Risk-app and source facts The "how much" — outputs of the risk computation systems and source booking systems, brought in before they get flattened into reports. fact_trade, fact_mtm, fact_limit, fact_limit_utilization, fact_collateral_balance, fact_margin_call, fact_margin_dispute, fact_sensitivities, fact_pfe, fact_market_data, fact_country_limit, fact_notional_monitoring, fact_counterparty_rating.
Layer 3 — CCR analytics view (Gold) The integration layer. Joins all dimensions to all facts in a way that supports the dimensional composition George wants. Direct exposure (counterparty → trades → MTM) and indirect exposure (party as collateral provider, issuer, related party) sit in the same view. Default risk computed as gross_mtm − collateral_haircut_adjusted + pfe_addon. PnL attribution computed as sensitivity × market_move, aggregated to counterparty / desk / asset class. Tenor-bucketed measures, period-comparison fields (DoD, WoW, MoM, QoQ) computed once, available to any consumer. This is the layer that doesn't fully exist today.
Layer 4 — BI consumption The rendering tier. Dashboards, drill paths, filters, conditional formatting, column pickers, tabbed dimensional navigation. The "easy part" — but only after Layers 1–3 exist. The BI tool is interchangeable. George's "tabs on top," "column picker," "context-sensitive columns" features get implemented here — they all become straightforward when the underlying model is dimensional.

4.3 The integration sequence

A defensible sequence:

  1. Foundation — establish the conformed dimensions in Databricks Silver. dim_party, dim_country, dim_product, dim_trade_purpose, dim_tenor_bucket, dim_line, dim_security, dim_party_relationship.
  2. Replace SCREAM-as-report with SCREAM-as-source — pipeline SCREAM's underlying trade and limit tables into Silver, conformed to the new dimensions. Re-platform the Legal Name View against the new model.
  3. Add Colline / CMOS — pipeline collateral data, integrate margin call detail. Email digest goes away. Default risk becomes computable.
  4. Solve the trade-type taxonomy — fix Deal Type. Separate product from purpose from lifecycle.
  5. Generalise country-of-risk — collapse Israeli and other jurisdictional pages into one parameterised view.
  6. Add sensitivities pipeline — bring valuation-engine outputs into Silver. Enables risk movement attribution for FX (replicating today's view dimensionally) and lays the groundwork for the other asset classes.
  7. Add market data domain — pipeline external market data into Silver as a conformed dimension. Joins with sensitivities to enable risk attribution.
  8. Extend risk attribution to Equities, Rates, Credit, Commodities — replicate the FX page paradigm, parameterised by asset class.
  9. Rebuild the BI front-end — tabbed dimensional navigation, column picker, context-sensitive columns. Smallest piece of work and only meaningful after the above is in place.

Closing thread

The most important sentence Reena observed during the session: "Since these are all canned reports from which data has been read, these dashboards are simply depicting whatever data was in those reports. So it is pretty rigid." That's the diagnosis. George showed us a working PowerBI build that has materially helped his team — and every limitation he raised is a limitation of the data being fed to PowerBI, not of PowerBI itself.

The corollary: changing BI tool does not solve the rigidity. Strategy, Tableau, AI/BI Genie — all would inherit the same constraints if pointed at the same SCREAM canned reports. The leverage is upstream. Build the conformed dimensional model in Databricks once; serve any BI tool from it. The dashboard George wants — dynamic, dimensionally composable, joinable across CMOS and SCREAM, parameterisable by country, surfacing all five exposure concepts, attributing risk movement across all asset classes — is not a different dashboard. It's the same dashboard sitting on a fundamentally different data layer.
