1. Purpose and audience

This document captures the analytics-layer thinking spawned by George Karahotzitis sharing the GMC workstream PowerBI requirements that they had previously pulled together. The intent is fourfold:

  1. Awareness check on the LDM. Make sure the aggregate-level view we've been building has placeholders for every concept George's requirements call out. If something is missing, surface it now.
  2. Document the requirements verbatim. So they don't get lost. They are exactly the kind of input that drives a CCR analytics layer.
  3. Document the architecture thinking. The principle that dimensions come from domain models, measures come from Risk apps, and the dimensional layer is where they marry — this is a governance pattern worth capturing.
  4. Define terminology. The requirements use a lot of acronyms and concepts that aren't universal. Where we don't have confirmed definitions, we mark "needs SME confirmation" rather than guess.

Audience: The data modeler / data architect responsible for the analytics layer (i.e., the person who will eventually build this), plus stakeholders like Eleanor who need to see the data-modeling-to-business-value chain. The document also serves as a template that scales to other Risk domains (LRM/Liquidity, Market Risk, Capital).

2. The GMC requirements — what George asked for and why each matters

George shared a set of 12 requirements that the GMC workstream had previously pulled together for their PowerBI build. These are typical CCR exposure dashboard requirements but worth examining one by one because each one anchors a specific conformed dimension or fact in the analytics layer.

The 12 requirements with business context

1Currency on the facility / exposure (USD or CAD).

Why it matters: TD reports in two reporting currencies. Every measure in the dashboard needs to be available in both. This isn't trivially "convert at end of day" — the conversion methodology (spot vs WMR vs internal rate) must be consistent. Implies a Currency dimension and FX-rate-aware measures.

2Current exposure (i.e., MTM).

Why it matters: The headline metric. Mark-to-market exposure of the derivative portfolio. Required at the netting set and at the trade level for drill-down. Sourced from the derivative valuation systems (RCAPS, XVALA, etc.) at end of day.

3Show the collateral — it's a credit mitigant. Differentiate IM and VM.

Why it matters: Net exposure = MTM minus collateral. To present credit risk fairly, the dashboard must show both gross and net positions, with IM (Initial Margin, posted upfront for future-loss protection) separated from VM (Variation Margin, posted to settle current MTM). Sourced from CMOS.

4Show: trading desk from IHMS for each trade, trader name, trading supervisor (from REDS-GRS).

Why it matters: Attribution. When credit risk shows a number, the dashboard must allow drill-down to "whose trade?" — desk, trader, supervisor. This requires Book/Desk dimension joined to Trade fact.

5Show lending manager for each facility.

Why it matters: For banking-book exposures (loan facilities, committed lines), the responsible relationship manager is needed. Note: facilities are banking-book, not derivative. This requirement reaches outside our current CCR/Collateral scope into a separate Risk domain.

6Add functionality to query by booking point — to identify IHC / CUSO / Europe (TDGF) / Asia / etc.

Why it matters: TD has multiple legal entities and geographic booking points. The same trade might book to TDS US (IHC = Intermediate Holding Company per US prudential rules) or to TDGF (TD Global Finance) or other entities. Regulatory reporting and capital allocation differ by booking point. Booking point must be a queryable dimension.

7Industry classification / SIC code — and a view for non-bank financial institutions (NBFI), Banks, Corporates, Hedge Funds, SSAs, Commercial, and FMTA.

Why it matters: Counterparty type drives risk policy. Concentration limits, eligible products, and reporting cuts all depend on counterparty industry classification. SSAs and Banks have different capital treatment than Hedge Funds and Corporates. The Counterparty dimension needs a Client Type attribute that maps to the standard sector taxonomy.

8Decompose exposure by SIM, non-SIM, energy, and credit.

Why it matters: Risk-type decomposition. SIMM (Standard Initial Margin Model — usually written SIM in dashboards) is the ISDA-defined regulatory IM methodology. Non-SIM is anything outside SIMM scope (often Schedule IM under UMR or other legacy methods). Energy and credit are asset-class slices that GMC particularly cares about — likely because they have distinct concentration concerns. Implies a Risk Type dimension.

9Portfolio statistics (include trending — e.g., YTD, QoQ, MoM):

  • OTC: Notional by product type · MtM by product type · Top exposures by client type (NBFI, Banks, etc.) · Top 10 changes in DoD changes in absolute terms
  • SFT: Notional by underlying obligor (e.g., UST, GOC) · MtM by underlying obligor · Top exposures by client type · Top 10 changes in DoD changes in absolute terms

Why it matters: This is the trend-and-rank view that risk managers run every morning. YTD (year-to-date), QoQ (quarter-over-quarter), MoM (month-over-month), DoD (day-over-day) trending requires a robust Date dimension and historical fact persistence. Top-N rankings are window functions — computationally expensive at query time, often pre-aggregated. The OTC vs SFT (Securities Financing Transactions, like repos) split is a standard product-type bifurcation. Issuer-level views for SFT are because repo risk is mostly about the underlying collateral issuer.

10Within CCR Line Details, add sensitivities (from SENTOR).

Why it matters: A CCR Line is the netting-set-level exposure summary that risk managers look at. Sensitivities (Delta, Gamma, Vega, sometimes by tenor bucket) tell you how exposure changes with market moves. Sourced from a dedicated sensitivities engine (SENTOR appears to be that engine — needs SME confirmation). This couples the analytics layer to a risk-engineering output.

11Add a page with stress testing — include stressed PFE and standard PFE and include the last load date (since stress is only produced monthly). Include MTM stress from RCS and also include any IA and again, last load date.

Why it matters: Stress testing has different cadence (monthly) than daily exposure. The dashboard must show "as-of-last-stress-date" alongside daily exposure so users understand they're looking at stale stress numbers. PFE (Potential Future Exposure) is the credit risk metric; stressed PFE applies stress scenarios to it. RCS (Risk Capital Studio) is the stress engine. Implies a Stress Fact with its own Date dimension separate from the Daily Date.

12Add a page with issuer risk — for repo, total notional by issuer (GOC, Gilt, US Agency, UST, etc.).

Why it matters: For Securities Financing Transactions (repos), the actual credit risk often sits with the issuer of the underlying collateral, not the repo counterparty. A repo where TD lent cash against UST collateral has UST issuer risk; same repo against an unrated corporate has corporate risk. Implies an Issuer dimension on Security Master plus a fact filtered to repo product types.

4. Layered architecture — domain models, risk apps, dimensional layer, BI

The thinking here matters. It establishes a governance principle that scales beyond CCR.

The pattern

Analytics dashboards (PowerBI, MicroStrategy, Tableau) sit at the top of a stack. Below them, the data needs to be organized in a way that's both performant for queries and faithful to the source-of-truth. The layered architecture we're describing has four conceptual tiers:

Tier 1 — Source systems (apps and data products)

The originating systems where data is born. Two flavors:

  • Domain data products (the "what" — entities and reference data): Trade Master, Party Master, Security Master, Book/Desk hierarchy, Market Data, Valuation. These are the nouns of the business. They populate dimensions.
  • Risk apps (the "how much" — calculated measures): CMOS (collateral), CRS (legacy capital), RCS (Risk Capital Studio — stress/PFE), XVALA (XVA), RCAPS (market risk capital), RAMS (market risk attribution), TCC (counterparty credit), SRLM (liquidity), ALFA (liquidity & funding). These are the calculators. They populate facts.
Tier 2 — Domain models (Silver layer)

Normalized, curated, governed models per domain. This is where our 8-aggregate CCR/Collateral LDM sits. The Silver layer:

  • Captures full audit trail and bitemporal versioning
  • Preserves source-of-truth lineage (TAMS authoritative for terms, CMOS for ops, etc.)
  • Resolves source-system inconsistencies into canonical entities
  • Is NOT optimized for analytics queries — it's optimized for fidelity

Each Risk domain has its own Silver model: CCR/Collateral (this work), Capital, XVA, Liquidity, Market Risk, etc. Each owns its measures and entities.

Tier 3 — Dimensional layer (Gold layer)

Subject-level facts and conformed dimensions. This is where the analytics architect designs the star schemas:

  • Conformed dimensions are shared across facts. The same Counterparty Dimension is used by Daily Exposure Fact, Stress Fact, Sensitivities Fact, etc. Conformance is enforced by the data modeling team — not by the analytics consumer.
  • Subject-level facts are scoped per measurement process. Daily Exposure is one fact. Margin Calls are another. Stress is another. They're separate because their grain, cadence, and sources differ.
  • Each dimension's source is explicit — Counterparty dim comes from Party Master domain, Security dim from Security Master domain, etc.
  • Each fact's measures' source is explicit — Daily Exposure measures come from CMOS, Stress measures come from RCS, Sensitivities measures from SENTOR, etc.
Tier 4 — Consumption layer (BI / dashboard / self-service)

Two patterns common here:

  • Direct on dimensional layer — BI tool queries star schemas directly. Best for governance, slower for unsophisticated users.
  • Flat consumption fact (denormalized) — a wide table built on top of the dimensional layer for ease of BI consumption. Best for self-service users in PowerBI / MicroStrategy where every join is friction. Sacrifices some flexibility for usability.

The right answer is usually both — the dimensional star schema is the system of record for analytics, and a flat consumption view is generated from it for the easiest BI experience.

The principle that makes this work

Dimensions come from domain models. Measures come from Risk apps. The dimensional layer is where they marry. Neither dimensions nor measures are invented at the analytics layer — they're sourced from authoritative places. The analytics layer is a marriage point, not an originator.

This principle has consequences:

  • If the Counterparty domain model changes (say, a new client-type taxonomy), the Counterparty Dimension automatically reflects the change. The analytics architect doesn't have to "decide" — they inherit.
  • If RCS produces a new stress measure (say, Reverse Stress Test), it appears as a new column on the Stress Fact. The analytics architect designs how it joins to dimensions but doesn't define the measure.
  • The data modeler's role is to enforce conformance — making sure the Counterparty Dimension is identical regardless of which fact joins to it. This is data governance work, not analytics work.

The architecture above is explicitly drawn for CCR. But the same pattern works for every Risk analytics domain. Specifically:

Each Risk domain follows the same pattern: source apps + domain data products → Silver → dimensional layer → BI. The conformed dimensions are shared across domains. Book/Desk dimension is universal.

This means: build conformed dimensions ONCE, use them everywhere. That's the data modeler's leverage — one well-designed Counterparty dimension serves all Risk analytics. Without conformance, every analytics build re-invents the same dimensions, with subtle inconsistencies that bite later.

7. Why this thinking matters (the governance value)

Three reasons this layered architecture is worth establishing as a TD-wide pattern, not just doing it ad-hoc per project:

Reason 1 — Clear ownership prevents reinvention

If three analytics teams (CCR, Liquidity, Market Risk) each build their own "counterparty dimension" with their own definition of client type, the result is three subtly different views of the same counterparty in three different dashboards. By the time someone notices, weeks of analysis have been done on inconsistent foundations. The conformed dimensional layer prevents this by saying "Counterparty is owned by Party Master domain, Counterparty dimension is built once, every fact joins to the same dimension."

Reason 2 — Source-system changes propagate predictably

When Party Master adds a new client_type value (say, "Family Office" gets split out from "Asset Manager"), the change propagates to the Counterparty dimension automatically, and from there to every dashboard that uses it. Without the layered architecture, every dashboard owner has to find out about the change and update their own copy. Data modelers handle the propagation; analytics consumers don't have to.

Reason 3 — It justifies the data modeling role itself

This is worth saying explicitly. In many shops, the data modeler is seen as a documentation function — "they draw diagrams." But the layered architecture surfaces the actual value: data modelers are the people who design the conformance contract between source systems and analytics. Without that contract, every BI build is a one-off integration. With it, BI builds are composable. That's the case to make to Eleanor.

What this means for our current work

Our 8-aggregate CCR/Collateral Silver LDM is the foundation. When we move to the analytics layer (later, after LDM is stable), we will:

  • Build conformed dimensions on top of Silver, sourced from Party Master, Trade Master, Security Master, Book/Desk, etc.
  • Build subject-level facts per measurement process (Daily Exposure, Margin Call, Collateral Position, Movement, Stress, Sensitivities, etc.)
  • Build a flat consumption fact (or several) for BI ease-of-use
  • Connect to PowerBI / MicroStrategy via the consumption layer

The Silver work we're doing now is what makes all of this possible. If Silver is incomplete or inconsistent, the analytics layer inherits that incompleteness. If Silver is solid, the analytics layer is a presentation problem, not a data problem.

Risk metrics and methodologies

PFEindustry standard

Potential Future Exposure. Statistical measure of how much exposure could grow over a future horizon at a given confidence level (e.g., 95th percentile). Standard CCR metric. "Stressed PFE" applies stress scenarios; "Standard PFE" uses base-case parameters.

MTMindustry standard

Mark-to-Market. Current value of a derivative based on current market prices. The starting point of CCR exposure measurement.

SIMM (or SIM)industry standard

Standard Initial Margin Model. ISDA-defined methodology for calculating Initial Margin under UMR (Uncleared Margin Rules). Most regulated bilateral derivatives use SIMM. "Non-SIM" generally means anything outside SIMM scope (Schedule IM, legacy methods).

IM / VMindustry standard

Initial Margin / Variation Margin. IM is collateral posted upfront to cover potential future losses (regulated by UMR). VM is collateral exchanged daily to settle current MTM moves.

IAindustry standard

Independent Amount. Pre-UMR concept — fixed amount of collateral posted independent of MTM. Largely superseded by SIMM IM under UMR but persists in legacy agreements and trade-level structures (synthetic prime brokerage).

SFTindustry standard

Securities Financing Transaction. Repos, reverse repos, securities lending, and similar product types. Distinct from OTC derivatives — different product economics, different risk profile (issuer risk vs counterparty risk).

CCR Lineindustry standard

The netting-set-level credit exposure summary for a counterparty. "Show me the CCR Line for Counterparty X" means show the aggregated MTM, IM, VM, PFE, and other measures across all trades with X under all governing agreements.

Issuers (for SFT issuer-risk analysis)

USTindustry standard

US Treasury. Sovereign issuer of US government bonds. Standard repo collateral.

GOCindustry standard

Government of Canada. Canadian sovereign issuer of CGB (Canada Government Bonds). Standard CAD-denominated repo collateral.

Giltindustry standard

UK government bond issued by HM Treasury. Standard GBP-denominated repo collateral.

US Agencyindustry standard

US government-sponsored enterprises (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, Ginnie Mae, etc.). Issuer credit is implicitly or explicitly backed by the US government. Common repo collateral.

Trending and time-series

YTD / QoQ / MoM / DoDindustry standard

Year-to-Date / Quarter-over-Quarter / Month-over-Month / Day-over-Day. Standard time-series comparisons. Each requires the fact table to retain enough history.
